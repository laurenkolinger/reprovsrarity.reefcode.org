About this study

This site accompanies a manuscript on how reproductive mode and rarity relate to coral population trajectories in the U.S. Virgin Islands. It presents four analyses, one per monitoring program: TCRMP, VINPS, and CSUN, which track coral cover on the reef, and the Lasker octocoral program, which tracks octocoral colony density.

The coral-cover data behind the TCRMP, VINPS, and CSUN analyses comes from the Reef Code benthic cover section. The species-level file backs the TCRMP and VINPS analyses: coral species cover download. The genus-level file backs the CSUN analysis: coral genera cover download. The octocoral analysis uses a separate, standalone collaborator dataset from the Lasker program, bundled with this site rather than drawn from the benthic cover section.

Two versions of each analysis

Each of the four analyses appears on this site in two versions. Open a program from the Publications menu to read the as-published version, then use the toggle at the top of the page to switch to the updated version and back.

The as published version reruns the analysis exactly as it appears in the manuscript. Each program clips the coral-cover or colony-survey data to the years used in the paper. TCRMP and VINPS clip to 2001 through 2023. CSUN clips to 1992 through 2023. The octocoral program clips to 2014 through 2024. A manuscript reports a result for a fixed dataset, so the published version locks the year range to match. Locking the year range is what lets a reader trace every number in the paper back to a specific, unchanging slice of the monitoring record, regardless of how much new data has been collected since.

The updated since publication version reruns the identical analysis: the same code, the same site scoping, and the same taxon scoping. Only the year range changes. It opens the year range to the full monitoring record bundled with this site. Monitoring for each program continues past the cutoff used in the paper, so the updated version extends the window to the most recent year available and lets the trajectories keep going. As new years are added to the underlying data files, the updated pages pick them up automatically the next time this site is rendered. The analysis itself does not change. Comparing the two versions side by side shows whether the pattern reported in the paper is holding, strengthening, or shifting as the monitoring record grows.

The monitoring programs

The analysis draws on three independent long-term stony-coral monitoring programs and one octocoral program, each with its own sites, depths, sampling design, and taxonomic resolution (Figure 1, Table 1). Combining programs that were built independently is a strength: when the same pattern emerges from methods that do not share equipment, observers, or reefs, the pattern is unlikely to be an artifact of any one design.

The Territorial Coral Reef Monitoring Program (TCRMP) has recorded benthic cover at 20 shallow (0 to 20 m) sites across all three islands since 2001 (1, 2). Each site is surveyed annually along six 10 m permanent transects filmed with a high-definition video camera held about 40 cm above the reef; non-overlapping images are annotated with 380 random points per transect, and corals are identified to species where possible. This analysis uses the shallow sites and the species-level identifications.

The Virgin Islands National Park Service (VINPS) program, part of the National Park Service South Florida/Caribbean Network, monitors 7 reefs off St. John and at Buck Island Reef National Monument, St. Croix, at 4 to 20 m depth (3–5). Each site carries 20 permanent 10 m transects filmed with a digital video camera; sequential still frames are annotated with 10 random points each, and substrata are classified to coral species. Sites are surveyed annually.

The California State University Northridge (CSUN) program is the longest record, running since 1992 at 8 sites on the fringing reefs between White Point and Cabritte Horn, St. John, at 7 to 9 m depth (6). Each site is sampled annually with random photoquadrats analyzed in CPCe or CoralNET; because image resolution and low cover limit species identification, CSUN corals are reported at genus level, so the CSUN analysis runs on genera.

The Lasker octocoral program is a supplementary comparison. It counts octocoral colonies in randomly placed 1 m² quadrats along six 10 m transects at three sites on the southern shore of St. John (Grootpan Bay, Europa Bay, and Tektite) at 7 to 9 m depth, from 2014 onward (7, 8). It measures colony density rather than percent cover and tests whether the reproductive-mode pattern seen in stony corals extends to a different cnidarian group on the same reefs.

Reproductive mode and rarity

Every taxon carries two labels: a reproductive mode and a historical-abundance class. Reproductive mode is brooder or broadcaster. For the species-level programs the assignment reads a compiled trait table (Mahoney) and fills any gaps from a second table (Olinger), a coalesce that prefers the primary source and falls back to the secondary. For the genus-level CSUN program, each genus is assigned the reproductive mode most common among its species in the region, from a genus-level trait table (Edmunds). Taxa that cannot be placed leave the analysis, because the model contrasts brooders with broadcasters and cannot position an unassigned taxon (Table 2).

Historical abundance is common or rare. Within each program, the taxa are ranked by mean cover over the baseline five-year period (2001 to 2005 for TCRMP and VINPS, 1992 to 1996 for CSUN), and the taxa that together hold the top 90 percent of cumulative baseline cover are common; the rest are rare. A sliding-window sensitivity analysis identified 90 percent as the strictest rarity threshold at which the temporal slopes stay stable. Baseline abundance is measured after the onset of regional coral decline that began in the 1980s (9–11), yet these baseline ranks resemble those described for Caribbean reefs in the 1950s and 1960s (12) and in the fossil record (13–15). Abundance is measured as percent cover, a standard metric for colonial organisms (11, 16), and the analysis models its log₁₀ so that slopes read as proportional annual rates of change (17, 18).

The integrated result

The headline result combines the three programs in one figure (Figure 2). Each program contributes a row: on the left, the baseline abundance distribution with every taxon colored by its group; on the right, the site-level group cover over time on a log scale, with a linear trend per group. Read across the three rows, broadcast-spawning corals decline steeply while brooding corals hold or rise, and within each reproductive mode the common and rare taxa fall at the same proportional rate. The pattern tracks reproductive mode, not rarity.

The model behind the figure is a linear model of log₁₀ cover on year, rarity, reproductive mode, and every interaction, fit separately for each program with HC3 robust standard errors (19). Table 3 reports the coefficients side by side. The three-way Year × Rarity × Reproductive-mode term is where a rarity effect that differs by reproductive mode would appear.

Occupancy versus extent

Two taxa can lose the same amount of cover in different ways: one by disappearing from sites (an occupancy loss), another by thinning where it persists (an extent loss). Figure 3 separates the two. Each arrow runs from a taxon’s baseline occupancy and extent to its recent values, colored by group. Broadcasters tend to slide down and to the left (thinner and less widespread); several brooders hold or expand their occupancy.

Brooders and broadcasters over time

Reproductive mode reshapes the community. Figure 4 follows the mean cover of broadcasters and brooders separately in each program, and tracks the brooder share of total coral cover. Broadcaster cover falls across all three programs while brooder cover holds steadier, so the brooder share of cover climbs over the record. In two programs it approaches the 50 percent parity line, meaning brooders may soon hold as much reef cover as broadcasters, a reversal of the historical dominance of broadcasting reef-builders.
